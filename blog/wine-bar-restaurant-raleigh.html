Wine at Crawford & Son

March 2026 • Stories

The wine program at Crawford & Son doesn't try to be everything. It's built on focus—France, Italy, Spain—and a curator's instinct for bottles that taste good with the food.

Jordan Joseph shapes the list with the kind of knowledge that shows when you ask questions. He knows why a particular producer matters. He knows the vintage. He can tell you what to drink with swordfish and what changes when the menu shifts. That's the difference between a list and a program.

The bottles reflect a simple philosophy: find producers who care, find wines that work, keep the selection lean. Champagne Josselin sits on the list—a grower producer from a small village, the kind of bottle that's minerally and bright and works as an aperitif. Barolo from Giacomo Conterno appears in the by-the-glass selection when it's ready, and when it arrives, you know it's tasted. That's the work. Not collecting. Curating.

By-the-glass selections rotate. The kitchen calls upstairs with what's coming—a rich dish, a delicate fish, vegetables—and the wine program responds. That's the whole point of the relationship between food and wine. It's not theoretical. It's practical. It's what lands on your table right now, designed to work with what you're about to eat.

Seasonal pairings reflect the menu's shifts. Spring brings lighter wines, higher acidity, brightness that matches new vegetables. Winter calls for something deeper, fuller—bottles that stand alongside braised dishes and darker preparations. The menu changes. The wine program moves with it.

Right next door, separated by a wall, is Bar Sous Terre—the sister wine bar that expanded the philosophy into its own space. It's Person Street. One restaurant, one wine bar, both built with the same attention. If you want more depth, more exploration of the list, more time with wine and small bites—that's the adjacent room. But the core idea is the same: wine that tastes good, selected with care, poured by people who understand it.

The wine program isn't separate from the restaurant. It's part of the whole experience. You sit down. You eat. You drink something that makes the food better. Someone thought about that before you arrived.
